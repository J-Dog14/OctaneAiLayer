# Pitcher Archetypes — Pattern Recognition

These are the mechanical archetypes we've identified across the 8ctane cohort. When analyzing a new pitcher, check which archetype(s) their data fits — it sharpens the diagnosis.

## The Model Arm

**Profile:** All 8 cylinders Strength or Elite. Healthy stress per mph (≤1.25 Nm/mph). Properly timed pelvis decel. Cyl 3 product 350+ via balanced sagittal × flex.

**Example: Max Boyatt (93.2 mph)**
- Stride 80.8% (Strength)
- HSS @ FS 63° (Elite)
- Cyl 3 product 357 (balanced 8.7 × 41)
- Knee ext 13° (Watch but acceptable)
- Trunk tilt 41° forward / 45° lateral (Elite)
- HzAbd dwell 40 ms (Strength)
- Back GRF 1.61 BW (Strength)
- Elbow torque 115 Nm / 1.23 Nm/mph (Healthy)
- Pelvis-to-trunk amplification 1.81× — best in cohort

**Coaching note:** Don't rebuild. Additive work only — careful, additive velocity intent. The risk of "improving" a model arm is breaking the chain efficiency.

## The Sequenced Thrower

**Profile:** Late pelvis (low PelvAV@FS, peak after FS), late arm (low ER@FS). Everything fires together as a compressed unit. High raw peak pelvis velocity but moderate HSS. Pelvis-to-trunk amplification typically below band (≤ 1.2×) because the trunk doesn't have a loaded spring to release through.

**Example: Dylan Stacks (91.7 mph)**
- Peak pelvis 914 °/s (#1 in cohort)
- PelvAV @ FS only 60 °/s, peak 45 ms after FS
- HSS @ FS 37° (low)
- Pelvis-to-trunk amplification 1.09× (worst in cohort)
- Hand ang vel #2 (chain rescue at the wrist)
- Stress per mph elevated at 1.36

**Coaching note:** Engine is there. Fix the TIMING. Get the pelvis firing earlier in the stride so HSS can build. The hand/arm don't need to change.

## The Rubber Band

**Profile:** Early pelvis (high PelvAV@FS, peak near/at FS), late arm (low ER@FS). Pelvis fires hard while trunk is held back — separation builds in the FS→MER window. Clean amplification.

**Examples: Fletcher, McLamb, sometimes Boyatt**

- Pelvis ang vel at FS: 200–500+ °/s
- HSS @ FS: 50–60°+
- Pelvis-to-trunk amplification ≥ 1.4×
- MER tends to lower (185–190°) because the stretch does the work

**Coaching note:** This is the elite-velocity archetype. Maintenance, not change.

## The Arm Thrower (Compensating)

**Profile:** Soft block (low Cyl 4), high stress per mph (≥1.35 Nm/mph), elevated humerus-to-hand amplification (≥ 1.0× — arm rescuing chain). Pelvis may be fine but the chain breaks at the trunk or front leg, dumping load on the arm.

**Examples:**
- JT Williams (127 Nm @ 86.8 mph = 1.46 Nm/mph). Soft block (8° knee ext), back-leg drive light (1.23 BW). Arm is paying the chain's tax.
- John McLamb (126 Nm @ 85.8 mph = 1.47 Nm/mph). Same family.

**Coaching note:** Address the upstream leak (block, drive leg, sequencing) before pushing for more velocity. Don't try to "fix the arm" — the arm is the symptom, not the cause.

## The Trunk-Dumper

**Profile:** Cyl 3 product high but driven by extreme forward flex (>50° at release) rather than balanced sagittal × flex. The trunk is compensating for an under-amplified rotational chain.

**Example: Carson Carroll (86.9 mph)**
- Cyl 3 product 472 (cohort highest!) — but driven by 59° forward flex × 8.0 mph sagittal
- Pelvis-to-trunk amplification only 1.14× (below band)
- Pelvis ang vel elite at peak (833) but chain doesn't transfer

**Coaching note:** The extreme trunk dump should moderate naturally if the sequencing improves. Connection-ball drills to widen the pelvis-trunk peak gap.

## The Short-Strider Compensator

**Profile:** Cyl 1 short (<75% of height), Cyl 2 hyper-separated (>65°), Cyl 5 trunk dumps to compensate, lower-half output below cohort despite reasonable mechanics elsewhere.

**Example: Isaac James (80.9 mph at 5'7"/160)**
- Stride only 68% (shortest in cohort)
- HSS @ FS 72° (hyper)
- Front leg extends 58° (extreme)
- Pelvis-to-trunk amplification 1.76× (elite — chain works despite frame)

**Coaching note:** The hyper-separation is a symptom of the short stride — the pelvis is rotating earlier and faster than ideal to find timing. Lengthen the stride first; the hyper-separation should moderate.

## The Quiet-Trunk

**Profile:** Cyl 3 product low (<200) with low forward flex (<25°) and modest sagittal velocity. Trunk barely contributes to the throw; velocity (whatever it is) is being produced primarily by pelvis + arm with the trunk as a passive link.

**Examples:**
- Carson Crider (85.2 mph, 15° forward flex, Cyl 3 product 101)
- Luke Johnson (77.9 mph, 1° forward flex, Cyl 3 product 7 — outlier flag)

**Coaching note:** The trunk needs to dump. Stride length development + front-leg block work usually pulls trunk flex along with it. Until then, the arm is taking deceleration load the trunk should be taking.

## The Sub-Max Capture (not really an archetype, but flag)

**Profile:** Shoulder distraction force < 0.5 BW, very tight velo SD (<0.3 mph), tight joint-angle SDs. Capture is at bullpen-effort intensity, not game effort.

**Examples:**
- Trevor Cleveland (0.49 BW shoulder distraction, 0.08 mph SD across 5 throws)
- Crider (0.56 BW)
- Wong (0.39 BW)

**Coaching note:** Real ceiling and stress profile can only be assessed at game-effort. Recommend re-test at competitive intent before drawing final conclusions.

## The Developmental Case

**Profile:** Significant flags across multiple cylinders, but elite raw rotational power suggesting the engine is there once the firing order is fixed. Usually young pitchers (16–17) with broken sequencing.

**Example: Luke Johnson (77.9 mph, 16.9 yrs)**
- Pelvis ang vel peak 989 °/s (cohort highest!)
- Hand ang vel 6,129 °/s (top of MLB range)
- BUT: HSS only 14°, MER only 155°, Cyl 3 product 7, pelvis ang vel @ FS = 6 (pelvis not loaded at landing), kinematic sequence trunk-before-pelvis

**Coaching note:** Don't chase velocity. Build the chain from the ground up. Mechanical re-wire before any velocity push.

## Reading patterns across cylinders

When you see a low Cyl 2 (HSS), check Cyl 3 amplification — it usually under-performs.

When you see high Cyl 8 stress per mph, check Cyl 4 (block) and Cyl 7 (drive) — usually one or both is weak, putting load on the arm.

When you see Cyl 6 dwell short, check the rest of Cyl 6 — the snapshot value matters too, but dwell is the better predictor.

When you see a sub-max capture indicator (low shoulder distraction), be cautious about declaring upper limits — the pitcher's true profile may differ at game effort.
