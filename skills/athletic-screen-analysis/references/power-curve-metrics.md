# Power Curve Metrics — Interpretation Reference

This reference applies to ALL assessments in the screen. Every assessment includes:
- A power curve graph (Mean Power trace vs. 10-90% rise line)
- A metrics table with Max RPD, Kurtosis, and Work (AUC)
- A radar chart showing percentile rankings across six variables

---

## The Power Curve Graph

The power curve shows normalized power output (y-axis, 0–1.0) over normalized time
(x-axis, 0–1.0). Two lines are plotted:

- **Mean Power (red/colored line):** The athlete's actual mean power output across trials,
  from ground contact to takeoff.
- **10-90% Rise Line (green/reference line):** A linear reference showing what an idealized
  smooth power ramp would look like if the athlete accelerated evenly from 10% to 90% of
  peak power.

### How to Read the Power Curve

**Curve above the reference line (especially early):** The athlete generates power rapidly
early in the movement. This is generally favorable — it indicates a high rate of power
development and explosive intent.

**Curve below the reference line:** The athlete is slow to develop power. Power accumulates
gradually rather than explosively. In reactive movements (DJ) this is a significant concern;
in loaded movements (CMJ) a slight lag can be normal.

**Curve shape — rise, plateau, and fall:**
- A sharp, narrow spike to peak followed by rapid drop = power concentrated at one moment.
  The athlete hits their peak and immediately loses force. Total work (AUC) will be lower.
- A gradual rise, sustained plateau near peak, then controlled descent = distributed power.
  The athlete stays in their power through the movement. Total work (AUC) will be higher.
- Irregular/jagged trace = inconsistent neuromuscular recruitment. Common in the PPU.
  Less common in lower-body movements — if you see it there, note it.

**When the curve rises very late (far right on the time axis):** Peak power is occurring
near the end of the movement. The athlete is a "late bloomer" in the movement — they ramp
slowly, which limits how much power is expressed during the key force application window.

## Max RPD (Maximum Rate of Power Development)

**What it measures:** The maximum rate at which power increases during the movement, measured
in watts per second (W/s). This is the steepest slope on the power curve — essentially, how
explosively the athlete can ramp up their power output.

**Why it matters:** In baseball, power events are extremely short in duration. The front leg
hits the ground in a split second. Hip rotation lasts a fraction of a second. Arm acceleration
is even shorter. The ability to produce power at a high RATE is often more important than the
ability to produce high peak power on its own, because the movement is over before a slow
power ramp can reach its peak.

**Thresholds by assessment:**
| Assessment    | Elite Threshold |
|--------------|----------------|
| Drop Jump    | >32,700 W/s    |
| CMJ          | >25,200 W/s    |
| Plyo Pushup  | >8,000 W/s     |
| Single Leg V | >18,200 W/s    |

**Interpretation:**
- **Elite Max RPD:** The athlete can ramp to peak power extremely fast. This is the quality
  that separates athletically explosive movers from those who are merely strong.
- **Below-elite Max RPD (especially in DJ or SLV):** The athlete's power development is too
  slow for the reactive demands of the movement. Even if their peak power is high, they can't
  access it quickly enough in sport-speed contexts.
- Always check Max RPD alongside Time to Max RPD (on the radar chart). A high Max RPD that
  occurs late in the movement (high Time to Max RPD percentile = occurring later) means the
  athlete accelerates powerfully but starts late — possibly a timing or intent issue.

## Kurtosis

**What it measures:** The statistical shape of the power curve. In this context, kurtosis
describes how "peaked" or "flat" the power distribution is across the movement.

**Why it matters:** A flat distribution (negative kurtosis) means power is spread relatively
evenly through the movement — the athlete applies force consistently, not just at one moment.
A sharp/peaked distribution (positive or less-negative kurtosis) means power is concentrated
at a single point, with rapid drop-off on either side.

**Thresholds and labels by assessment:**

| Assessment    | Ideal Range     | Label if Too Flat | Label if Too Sharp/Peaked |
|--------------|-----------------|-------------------|-----------------------------|
| Drop Jump    | -1.45 to -1.1   | Very Flat         | Sharp / Peaked              |
| CMJ          | -1.3 to -0.4    | Very Flat         | Sharp / Peaked              |
| Plyo Pushup  | -1.55 to -1.25  | Very Flat         | Sharp / Peaked              |
| Single Leg V | -1.0 to -0.2    | Very Flat         | Sharp / Peaked              |

**Interpretation:**

- **Within the ideal range:** The curve shape is appropriate for the movement. Power builds
  and sustains at a level that allows for effective force application through the full ROM.
- **Too flat (very negative kurtosis):** Power is spread very broadly — the athlete may be
  ramping slowly and never producing a sharp enough peak. Can indicate the athlete is not
  generating a focused, explosive contraction.
- **Too peaked/sharp (kurtosis above the upper bound):** Power spikes and collapses. The
  athlete has a narrow window of peak output and drops off steeply. This reduces total work
  done (AUC will typically be below ideal too). In pitching, this means force is applied for
  a very short window and the movement degrades quickly after the peak.
- **Positive kurtosis (sharp):** Particularly notable. This indicates a very sharply peaked
  curve — power is concentrated even more narrowly. Most common in the SLV when seen, and
  often associated with below-ideal AUC.

## Work / AUC (Area Under the Curve)

**What it measures:** The total power output integrated across the entire movement — essentially
the total work done by the athlete from ground contact (or start of movement) to takeoff
(or end of push). Reported in arbitrary units normalized for comparison.

**Why it matters:** Peak power tells you the best single moment. AUC tells you how much
power was produced throughout the whole movement. An athlete with high peak power but low
AUC produces a power spike but can't sustain it. An athlete with high AUC maintains force
application through the full range of motion — which in pitching means the front leg doesn't
"give" early, the hip doesn't stop rotating before full extension, and the arm doesn't
decelerate before release.

**Thresholds by assessment:**
| Assessment    | Ideal Threshold | Tier Labels (examples) |
|--------------|----------------|------------------------|
| Drop Jump    | >570            | Below Average / Above Average / Elite |
| CMJ          | >1,260          | Below Average / High / Elite          |
| Plyo Pushup  | >700            | Below Average / High / Elite          |
| Single Leg V | >1,030          | Below Average / High / Elite          |

**Interpretation:**

- **Elite AUC:** The athlete applies force through a long, sustained window. Power doesn't
  spike and die — it builds, sustains, and maintains through the movement. This is the quality
  that allows the kinetic chain to stay loaded all the way through ball release.
- **High (but not elite) AUC:** Solid sustained power. There is room to develop, but this
  is not a primary limiting factor.
- **Below average AUC (especially alongside high peak power):** The most important pattern
  to flag. The athlete generates impressive peaks but can't hold them. This tells you their
  power is event-driven rather than sustained. Training interventions that emphasize staying
  in the power position — longer time under tension at high velocities, contrast training,
  full-ROM explosive movements — are indicated.

## Radar Chart

The radar chart shows six variables simultaneously on a percentile scale (20th, 40th, 60th,
80th, 100th). Variables are:
- Jump Height
- Peak Power (Norm)
- Work (AUC)
- Kurtosis
- Max RPD
- Time to Max RPD

**How to read it:**

The radar chart is your quickest summary tool. A well-rounded athlete has a large, relatively
even polygon. A specialist or an athlete with specific gaps shows a misshapen polygon —
spiking in some areas, pulling inward in others.

**Key patterns to identify:**

- **High Jump Height + High Peak Power, but low AUC:** Power is there, but it's a spike, not
  a sustained output. The athlete is explosive but brief.
- **High Max RPD + low Time to Max RPD:** The athlete ramps to power extremely fast and does
  so early in the movement. This is ideal for reactive assessments.
- **High Max RPD + high Time to Max RPD:** Fast ramp, but it happens late. The athlete
  accelerates well once they get going but starts slow. Could indicate a slow initiation
  or a long loading phase.
- **Low Kurtosis percentile:** On the radar chart, a low kurtosis percentile means the
  kurtosis value is very negative (very flat curve). This may or may not be ideal depending
  on whether it falls within the ideal range for that assessment — reference the specific
  assessment's kurtosis threshold.
- **Even radar with moderate scores everywhere:** The athlete is consistent but has no
  elite quality. No single variable stands out as a limiting factor — overall development
  is the priority.

**For the SLV specifically**, two radar traces are plotted (left and right). Compare the
shapes: if one leg has a distinctly different shape than the other (e.g., one is AUC-dominant
and the other is RPD-dominant), note this — it may reflect different compensation strategies
on each leg.

## Reading Patterns Across Assessments

When all four assessments are present, look at the power curve metrics as a set:

**Consistently low AUC across all movements:**
The athlete's global pattern is peak-and-drop. This is not assessment-specific — it's a
quality they need to develop across the board. Sustained force application training should
be a central theme of their programming.

**AUC good in lower body, poor in upper body (PPU):**
The upper half is the bottleneck for sustained power. Lower-body chain is doing its job;
the upper half can't match it. Upper-body explosive training with a full-ROM emphasis is
the priority.

**Max RPD elite in bilateral (DJ/CMJ) but below elite in SLV:**
The athlete can develop power fast bilaterally but loses this quality on a single leg. This
is a single-leg stability and coordination issue — the nervous system can't ramp power as
quickly when balance is challenged.

**Kurtosis consistently outside ideal range (too sharp) across multiple tests:**
The athlete is a "peaker" — they have a very narrow explosive window in everything they do.
This is a neuromuscular pattern, not a strength issue. Exposure to more prolonged high-power
outputs (contrast training, reactive strength circuits, loaded carries) may help broaden the
power curve across movements.
