# Single Leg Vertical (SLV) — Interpretation Reference

## Protocol

The athlete performs a single-leg vertical jump on each leg. **Arm use is permitted** — the
athlete can use their arms to generate momentum. This is intentional: Joey wants to assess
how the athlete functions as a whole athlete on a single leg, not artificially restrict them
to pure leg power in isolation. The SLV captures single-leg stability, power, and the ability
to coordinate full-body momentum through one leg.

Multiple trials may be collected per leg. Reported values represent the best trial for each leg.

## Why the SLV Matters

The SLV is a window into single-leg explosiveness and stability — qualities that are
absolutely fundamental to pitching. Nearly every critical moment in a pitch happens on
one leg: drive-leg push-off, stride, front-foot landing and bracing, and the follow-through.
An athlete who produces impressive bilateral power (CMJ) but struggles on a single leg has
a stability and coordination gap that will limit how efficiently they can transfer force
through the delivery.

The SLV also isolates left from right, allowing direct comparison between the **plant leg**
(lead/front leg in the delivery — for a right-handed pitcher, the left leg) and the **drive leg**
(the back leg on the rubber — for a right-handed pitcher, the right leg).

## Interpreting Left-Right Asymmetry in Pitchers

**The plant leg (lead leg) should be expected to outperform the drive leg for pitchers.**
This is not a red flag — it is a logical outcome of the demands the sport places on each leg.

The plant leg hits the mound, absorbs ground reaction forces, braces, and extends hundreds
of times per week across games, bullpen sessions, and catch play. This repeated high-force
reactive stimulus naturally develops the plant leg's stability and explosiveness over time.
The plant leg is the leg that the sport trains hardest — it cannot help but adapt.

When the plant leg outperforms the drive leg:
- Note this as expected and sport-specific.
- Do not frame it as a weakness or asymmetry to "fix."
- The goal is not for the drive leg to catch the plant leg — if it never does, that is fine.
- The goal is for **both legs to improve** over time through equal training.

**Training philosophy:**
Both legs are trained identically for single-leg stability, strength, and explosiveness.
There is no emphasis on "catching up" the drive leg to the plant leg — that logic would
require either over-training the drive leg or neglecting the plant leg, both of which are
counterproductive. The plant leg's advantage reflects sport adaptation, not a training gap.

**When to flag asymmetry:**
If the drive leg is dramatically underperforming across multiple metrics (jump height, peak
power, Max RPD, AUC) — especially at the beginning of training — flag it as a baseline to
track. The goal over time is to see both legs improve together, and large absolute gaps in
raw power (not just sport-adaptive percentile differences) can indicate a true strength or
stability issue that should be addressed through programming.

## Key Metrics and Thresholds

### Jump Height (per leg)
- Higher = better. Compare each leg separately to the distribution.
- Left and right are shown as separate percentiles.
- Note any significant absolute difference in values (not just percentile).

### Peak Power (Normalized, per leg)
- Normalized to bodyweight. Both legs reported separately.
- 90th+ = elite. 80th+ = above average. Below 60th = room to develop.

### Force at PP (per leg, percentile gauge)
- Green = elite, Yellow = moderate, Red = below average.
- Higher force at PP indicates greater ground reaction force generation on a single leg.

### Velocity at PP (per leg, percentile gauge)
- Green = elite, Yellow = moderate, Red = below average.
- Higher velocity = moving faster through the jump. Important for explosive single-leg actions.

### Power Curve Metrics (per leg)
| Variable   | Elite/Ideal Threshold | Notes |
|------------|----------------------|-------|
| Max RPD    | >18,200 W/s          | Elite single-leg rate of power development |
| Kurtosis   | -1.0 to -0.2         | Ideal curve shape — sharp kurtosis (outside range) means power is too concentrated |
| Work (AUC) | >1,030               | Elite single-leg total work — sustained force application |

**Note on Kurtosis in the SLV:**
The ideal kurtosis range (-1.0 to -0.2) is less flat than in bilateral movements, reflecting
the inherently more ballistic nature of a single-leg jump. "Sharp" kurtosis (positive values)
indicates power concentrated in a very narrow window — the athlete spikes to peak power and
drops off quickly. This limits how much total work is done through the movement, even if peak
power is impressive.

## Force-Velocity Profile in the SLV

Interpret the same way as bilateral assessments, but note that single-leg values will
naturally be lower in absolute terms. Focus on the percentile gauges for each leg:

**Both legs balanced (both force and velocity in green):** Excellent. The athlete has
well-rounded single-leg power on both sides.

**Force-dominant on one or both legs:** Strength is there, but speed of force application
is limited. This will show up as the athlete being able to jump but not doing so as
explosively as their strength would suggest.

**Significant force difference between legs (not explained by plant/drive leg adaptation):**
If the drive leg's force gauge is substantially below the plant leg's AND the absolute
jump height gap is large, note it as a monitoring point — not a crisis, but worth tracking.

## Radar Chart in the SLV

The SLV radar chart overlays both left and right legs on the same chart. This visualization
is especially useful for seeing:
- Where both legs are strong together (sectors where both traces push outward)
- Where one leg consistently outperforms the other
- Whether a high peak power percentile is matched by high AUC (sustained) or whether
  the spike is isolated (suggesting a peak-and-drop pattern)

Pay particular attention to Max RPD differences between legs — a large RPD gap often explains
jump height gaps even when peak power gauges look similar.

## SLV as a Pitching Performance Indicator

For a pitcher, single-leg performance tells you whether the bilateral power seen in the CMJ
and DJ can actually be expressed when the body is on one leg in a dynamic environment. It is
common for athletes to show impressive bilateral numbers but lose significant power output
going to a single leg — this indicates that stability and single-leg coordination are the
limiting factor, not raw strength.

The plant leg's landing, bracing, and extension are among the most physically demanding
single-leg actions in baseball. An elite plant leg profile (high jump height, high peak power,
elite Max RPD, elite AUC) means the front side of the delivery can act as a true foundation
for the kinetic chain — absorbing the stride force, holding against it, and directing it
upward through trunk rotation and arm acceleration. A weak plant leg profile means energy
is "leaking" at landing and the chain is disrupted before it reaches the arm.
