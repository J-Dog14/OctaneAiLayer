# Plyo Pushup (PPU) — Interpretation Reference

## Protocol

The athlete performs a clapping or explosive pushup from a force plate. The "jump height"
metric captures how high the hands leave the surface — essentially the upper-body equivalent
of a vertical jump. This is an explosive, ballistic upper-body assessment.

## Why the Plyo Pushup Matters

The PPU is the only direct assessment of upper-body explosive power in this screen. Joey
considers it his favorite upper-body assessment precisely because it exposes a gap that most
strength-focused athletes don't know they have.

The vast majority of athletes who train their upper body do so with sets, reps, and load —
bench press, rows, shoulder work. This builds force capacity (strength), but it does not
train the nervous system to produce that force rapidly. The result is a predictable profile
that shows up consistently: **high force at PP, low velocity at PP** — force-dominant.
Athletes who have specifically trained for upper-body explosiveness (ballistic medicine ball
work, loaded jumps, speed-emphasis pressing) will show a more balanced or velocity-leaning
profile, and those athletes genuinely stand out on this test.

For pitching, upper-body elastic capacity and speed-strength are directly linked to arm
acceleration. A pitcher who can produce force at the upper half quickly and elastically is
able to accelerate the arm more efficiently through the delivery. The PPU doesn't measure
arm specifically, but it captures the underlying quality: can the upper half produce and
express power explosively?

## Key Metrics and Thresholds

### Jump Height (Hand Height Off Surface)
- The height the hands achieve off the plate. Comparable to lower-body jump height in concept.
- 52nd percentile = average. This metric alone doesn't tell the full story — pair it with
  peak power and force-velocity profile.

### Peak Power (Normalized)
- Normalized to bodyweight. Directly comparable.
- 93rd percentile = elite upper-body explosive output.
- This is the single most important metric in the PPU for assessing explosive capacity.

### Force at PP (Percentile gauge)
- Green = elite, Yellow = moderate, Red = below average.
- 83rd percentile = strong force production. This is typical for athletes who strength train.

### Velocity at PP (Percentile gauge)
- Green = elite, Yellow = moderate, Red = below average.
- 65th percentile = moderate. This is the gap most strength-trained athletes show.
- **Low velocity at PP is the most common finding.** It means the athlete has the strength
  to produce force but isn't converting it into fast movement. The upper half moves powerfully
  but slowly — which is inefficient for pitching arm mechanics.

### Power Curve Metrics
| Variable   | Ideal Threshold | Notes |
|------------|----------------|-------|
| Max RPD    | >8,000 W/s     | Elite threshold is achievable — above this is elite upper-body rate of force development |
| Kurtosis   | -1.55 to -1.25 | Ideal shape: moderately flat, distributed power through the movement |
| Work (AUC) | >700           | Total work. Below this = athlete peaks but doesn't sustain force through full press range |

## The Typical PPU Power Curve

Joey notes that the power curve in the PPU is "usually a mess" — meaning the mean power
trace is often irregular, with a clear spike followed by a sharp drop rather than the smooth
ramp seen in the lower body assessments. This reflects the more complex motor pattern and
the fact that upper-body ballistic training is rarer. When you see a jagged or irregular
power curve, note it — it suggests the athlete is not smoothly accelerating through the
movement. A cleaner, smoother curve with a well-defined peak indicates better neuromuscular
coordination in the upper body.

## Force-Velocity Profile Interpretation

**Force-dominant (most common — high force, lower velocity):**
This is the default profile for strength-trained athletes who have not emphasized explosiveness.
They have the muscle to produce force but not the neuromuscular speed to apply it quickly.
For pitching, this is a limiting quality — arm acceleration is a high-velocity event, and
upper-body speed-strength needs to match the demand. Training emphasis: medicine ball throws,
ballistic pressing, upper-body plyometric progressions.

**Balanced or velocity-leaning (less common — indicates explosive training):**
Athletes with this profile have specifically developed upper-body speed-strength. This is
the more advantageous profile for a pitcher. Note it as a strength.

**Velocity-dominant with low force:**
Rare. This athlete moves quickly but lacks the strength base. Upper-body strength development
would directly translate to higher peak power.

## PPU as a Humbling Assessment

Athletes who consider themselves physically elite often have their assumptions challenged here.
High bench press numbers do not translate to high PPU performance because the PPU demands
ballistic expression of force — something that training with slow, maximal loads does not
develop. When an athlete scores poorly on velocity at PP despite strong force numbers, this
is a clear signal that their training has been missing the speed-strength component. This is
not a criticism of the athlete — it's a coaching gap that, once addressed, tends to respond
quickly to targeted explosive upper-body training.

## Connecting PPU to Pitching

The kinetic chain in pitching runs from the drive leg through the hips, trunk, shoulder,
and arm. The upper body's job is to receive energy from the trunk, redirect it, and accelerate
the arm through release. An upper body that can only produce force slowly becomes a bottleneck
in this chain. The PPU directly tests whether the upper half can contribute its part of the
chain explosively. Low velocity at PP in the PPU — especially paired with high peak power —
suggests the athlete has the raw material but hasn't developed the explosiveness to fully
participate in a high-velocity delivery.
