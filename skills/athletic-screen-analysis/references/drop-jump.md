# Drop Jump — Interpretation Reference

## Protocol

The athlete drops off a 12-inch box and immediately jumps as high as possible. Three trials
are collected. Trials 1 and 2 are uncoached — the athlete performs them however they see fit.
Trial 3 is cued. The cue depends on what the coach observes:

- **Default cue (most athletes):** "Be as quick off the ground as possible into the vertical."
  This cue is used because most athletes naturally take too long on the ground, and this cue
  typically produces their best trial by improving the trade-off between contact time and height.
- **Inverted cue (rare — for athletes who are already very quick but whose vertical suffers):**
  "Take a little longer but give me your best possible height." Used when the athlete's
  reactive speed is already elite and sacrificing a fraction of contact time would yield
  a meaningfully better jump height.

The reported values represent the best trial across all three.

**Arm use is permitted.** The athlete is allowed to use their arms and upper body to generate
momentum. This is intentional — Joey wants to see how the athlete coordinates their whole body,
not just their legs in isolation. The CMJ handles isolated lower body; the DJ is a full-body
reactive test.

## Why the Drop Jump Matters (and Why It's Weighted Heavily)

The drop jump is the most heavily weighted assessment in this screen for two reasons:

1. **It mimics the front-leg landing in pitching.** When a pitcher strides and plants, the
   front foot hits the mound, and the front leg must react to that ground contact, brace
   against it, and extend — all in a fraction of a second. The DJ captures this quality
   directly: the athlete is reacting to a sudden ground contact and must produce maximal
   force in minimal time.

2. **It reveals whole-body athleticism.** Because arms are allowed, the DJ shows whether
   the athlete can synchronize their upper and lower body explosively. An athlete who is
   strong in isolation (CMJ) but can't coordinate that strength into a reactive whole-body
   movement will show up here.

## Key Metrics and Thresholds

### Jump Height
- Higher = better. Compare to percentile distribution.
- The 89th percentile and above is elite. Below 50th is a clear gap.

### Peak Power (Normalized)
- Normalized to bodyweight, so directly comparable across athletes.
- Compare to percentile. 70th+ is solid, 80th+ is above average, 90th+ is elite.

### RSI (Reactive Strength Index — displayed as RSIx2)
- **Critical:** The RSI values on this report are multiplied by 2 to make change more
  visible over time. So a displayed value of 3.8 = a true RSI of 1.9. Joey's target
  for his athletes is a displayed RSI above 3. Athletes can approach a displayed value
  of 5 in elite cases.
- RSI captures the ratio of jump height to contact time. It is the single best indicator
  of how well an athlete uses the stretch-shortening cycle in a reactive context.
- RSI at 93rd percentile = exceptional reactive ability.

### Contact Time (CT)
- **Counterintuitive display:** Lower percentile = shorter contact time = BETTER.
  The distribution shows that this athlete has a SHORTER contact time than X% of the database.
  A 14th percentile CT means the athlete has a very SHORT contact time — this is elite.
- Target: approximately 0.33 seconds.
- CT and RSI must be interpreted together. A short CT paired with high RSI = truly elite
  reactive strength. A short CT paired with low jump height = quick but not powerful enough.
  A long CT paired with high jump height = strength is there but reactivity needs work.

### Force at PP (Percentile gauge)
- Green = elite, Yellow = moderate, Red = below average.
- High force at PP = the athlete is producing strong ground reaction forces.

### Velocity at PP (Percentile gauge)
- Green = elite, Yellow = moderate, Red = below average.
- High velocity at PP = the athlete is moving quickly through the movement.
- Most ideal: both force and velocity in the green.
- Force-dominant profile: high force, lower velocity — strong but not fast enough through
  the movement. Training emphasis: speed-strength, plyometric velocity work.
- Velocity-dominant profile: high velocity, lower force — quick but lacks the power base.
  Training emphasis: strength development.

### Power Curve Metrics (from reference table)
| Variable   | Elite Threshold | Notes |
|------------|-----------------|-------|
| Max RPD    | >32,700 W/s     | Rate of power development — how fast power is produced |
| Kurtosis   | -1.45 to -1.1   | Shape of power curve — see power-curve-metrics.md |
| Work (AUC) | >570            | Total work done — sustained force application |

## Common Profiles and What They Mean

**Elite RSI + low CT + high jump height:** Truly elite reactive athlete. The front leg in
pitching will be a weapon — it can absorb and redirect ground reaction forces with minimal
energy loss. This is the profile you want to see in a high-velo pitcher.

**High jump height + long CT + moderate RSI:** The athlete has power but isn't reactive.
They take too long to transition from landing to launch. The front leg will "bleed" time
during the plant phase, limiting how quickly force can be transferred up the chain.

**High RSI + low CT + moderate jump height:** Quick off the ground but not generating
enough peak power. Improving jump height (strength development) would improve RSI even
further and translate to better energy transfer through the kinetic chain.

**Below-average AUC despite good peak power:** The athlete spikes quickly but doesn't
sustain force through the full range. In pitching, this manifests as front-leg "giving"
too early — the leg loads and fires but doesn't maintain the stiffness needed through
ball release.

**Force-dominant F-V profile:** Very common in strength-trained athletes. They have the
strength but lack the velocity quality. Plyometric and speed-strength work (lower loads,
faster movements) would help balance the curve and improve peak power expression.
