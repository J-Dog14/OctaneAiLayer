# Counter Movement Jump (CMJ) — Interpretation Reference

## Protocol

The athlete starts flat-footed with hands on hips. They load into a countermovement (dip)
and jump as high as possible. **Hands remain on hips throughout** — this is a pure lower-body
power assessment with no upper-body contribution. This isolation is what distinguishes the
CMJ from the Drop Jump.

Multiple trials may be collected. Reported values represent the best trial.

## Why the CMJ Matters

The CMJ is the cleanest measure of lower-body power in this screen. Because the arms are
removed from the equation, the CMJ isolates the legs and hips entirely. This serves two
purposes:

1. **It establishes a lower-body power baseline** that can be compared to the DJ. If an
   athlete's DJ significantly outperforms their CMJ, the DJ improvement comes from their
   ability to coordinate their upper body and generate momentum — a sign of good whole-body
   athleticism. If the CMJ and DJ are close, it may suggest the athlete isn't using their
   arms effectively on the DJ, or that their lower-body power is genuinely the dominant factor.

2. **It directly maps to drive-leg power in pitching.** Hip extension and lower-body drive
   off the rubber are powered by the same qualities tested here: countermovement loading,
   peak power production, and the ability to transfer that force upward through the kinetic chain.

## Key Metrics and Thresholds

### Jump Height
- Higher = better. Compare to percentile.
- 80th+ is above average, 90th+ is elite.

### Peak Power (Normalized)
- Normalized to bodyweight. Directly comparable across athletes.
- 80th+ is solid, 90th+ is elite.

### Force at PP (Percentile gauge)
- Green = elite, Yellow = moderate, Red = below average.
- In the CMJ, force at PP represents how much ground reaction force the athlete can generate
  at the moment of peak power expression. 50th percentile is average.
- Athletes with lower force at PP relative to high velocity at PP are velocity-dominant —
  they move quickly but lack a strong enough strength base.

### Velocity at PP (Percentile gauge)
- Green = elite, Yellow = moderate, Red = below average.
- Higher velocity at PP means the athlete is moving faster through their jump at the moment
  of peak power. 76th+ is above average.
- A high-velocity, lower-force profile in the CMJ often indicates an athlete who is
  naturally quick but undertrained in pure strength.

### Power Curve Metrics
| Variable   | Elite/Ideal Threshold | Notes |
|------------|----------------------|-------|
| Max RPD    | >25,200 W/s          | Rate of power development — how explosively power is generated |
| Kurtosis   | -1.3 to -0.4         | Ideal curve shape — flat enough to show sustained power |
| Work (AUC) | >1,260               | Total work done through the movement |

## Force-Velocity Profile in the CMJ

The CMJ typically reveals one of three patterns:

**Balanced (both force and velocity in green):** The ideal. The athlete is both strong and
fast in their lower body. This profile translates to powerful, efficient hip drive on the mound.

**Force-dominant (high force, lower velocity):** The athlete is strong but not generating
that strength quickly enough. This is common in heavy strength-trained athletes who haven't
incorporated enough speed-strength or plyometric work. For pitching, this means the drive
leg has the capacity but doesn't express it at the speed needed for maximal kinetic chain
contribution.

**Velocity-dominant (high velocity, lower force):** The athlete is quick but lacks the
strength base. This means their jump height is limited by force production, and their lower
half will be a limiting factor in generating and transferring power up the chain.

## What Low AUC Means in the CMJ

The CMJ provides one of the most reliable AUC readings because the movement is controlled
and standardized. An AUC below the ideal threshold (>1,260) tells you the athlete loads and
fires well but cannot sustain force application through the full movement. In pitching terms,
this often correlates to a drive leg that initiates hip rotation powerfully but bleeds force
before full extension — limiting how much energy gets transferred to the trunk and arm.

## CMJ vs. DJ Cross-Reference

Always compare CMJ and DJ when both are present:

- **DJ jump height significantly exceeds CMJ jump height:** The athlete's arms and whole-body
  coordination add meaningfully to their output. Good sign of athletic coordination.
- **CMJ jump height is close to or exceeds DJ jump height:** Unusual. May indicate the athlete
  isn't leveraging their arms on the DJ, or that reactive (stretch-shortening) ability is a
  limiting factor — the athlete is stronger in a controlled movement than in a reactive one.
- **CMJ RSI/AUC profile is strong but DJ work (AUC) is weak:** The athlete can produce power
  in isolation but loses it when coordination is required — a whole-body timing issue.
